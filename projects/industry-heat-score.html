Industry Heat Score

A monthly refreshed industry ranking that changes what enters the lead queue, not who reps prioritize within it. The model shifts list-building upstream toward industries with stronger customer durability and acquisition momentum, with $1M-$8M modeled annualized New MRR upside.

Role: Sole modeler & PRD author Stack: BigQuery, Dataform, Python audit harness Period: 2026 Q2

Problem

The sales team needed to improve lead quality without simply telling reps to skip broad swaths of assigned leads. The operational constraint was clear: reps still needed sufficient volume, but the input mix could be improved upstream.

  • Rep workflows needed enough lead volume to remain useful.
  • Moderate-quality leads still had operational value in the sales motion.
  • The strongest lever was lead-supply quality, not rep triage.

The real lever is upstream of the rep: change what gets loaded into the program in the first place.

Approach

Two-stage scoring architecture: an A-floor that gates out industries with poor customer durability, then a B-rank that scores survivors on acquisition momentum.

flowchart LR
  Universe[~300 industry codes] --> AFloor["A-floor: customer durability filter"]
  AFloor --> BRank[B-rank: acquisition momentum scoring]
  BRank --> Output[Ranked target list + expected conversion signal]
  Output --> SQL[List-build filter]

Filter-then-rank is more defensible than weighted scoring across both dimensions because the A-floor is a gate (binary), not a weight. Industries that fail durability don't get a partial-credit ranking — they're out.

Why Industry-Level

Industry-level for v1, account-level for v2: the roughly 300-code industry universe was small enough to score, audit by inspection, and route directly into existing list-building logic. If industry-level signal proved weak, the audit would still de-risk any future account-level scoring work.

Build vs buy rationale: the custom approach used proprietary internal customer outcomes rather than generic web-behavior intent. That made it more directly tied to the business motion and easier to validate against historical performance.

What I built

  • Full PRD: one-paragraph version, problem framing, scope/out-of-scope/non-goals, methodology, A-floor + B-rank construction, backtest design, sprint plan, integration plan, risks & mitigations
  • Gate-first sprint plan: initial audit checked coverage, sample size, and between-segment lift before committing to a full build: 70%+ coverage, 30+ viable codes, and 1.5x+ top-vs-bottom lift.
  • Audit harness: repeatable Python workflow that runs the validation checks, writes artifacts, and prints pass/fail results.
  • Companion model card: clarified how upstream list-building and downstream rep triage models complement each other.

Impact

  • Modeled annualized New MRR upside from shifting lead mix toward higher-converting industries at constant lead volume: $1M-$3M conservative and $2M-$8M optimistic.
  • Provided an internal-signal alternative to a generic third-party intent purchase, avoiding a likely $100K-$300K annual platform spend.
  • Project framing — "upstream of the rep, not the rep" — became a reusable pattern for B2B GTM prioritization questions.

Lesson

When direct prioritization advice is hard to operationalize, the right move is often to re-scope the problem one level upstream. An explicit go/no-go gate is also one of the cheapest ways to de-risk a model before building for weeks.
